Merchant Plus - Payment Pages

Merchant Plus (M+) is the new and improved Merchant Dashboard (MD)

Duration: 3 weeks
Team/Role: Sole designer (with help from team)
Size: Hot fix -> XL
Status: Partially live

BACKGROUND

Merchants are upset there's no Payment pages

After launching M+, we got bombarded with complaints about how merchants can‚Äôt see their payout information or how their store is performing financially.

Screenshot of the payment-related pages (2 total) on MD

THE TASK

Where to migrate payment pages to M+

I was tasked with the hot-fix solution to find somewhere on M+ to migrate the two payment-related pages, which are currently nested under the Account Settings tab on MD.

Current placement on MD vs. shipped placement on M+

Previous iterations

One of the suggestions was to nest it under the ‚ÄòSettings‚Äô page, similiar to the approach on MD. My counter-arguments to this approach were:

Core experience: recieving payment is so crucial to any type/size of merchant, making it as easily accessible as possible will reduce friction
Unintuitive and hidden: because payment is a core experience, it should be easy to find to and pass the 3-click rule
Scaling up in the future: if we start nesting things under ‚ÄòSettings‚Äô without strong design rationale, then we may start dumping more random stuff and overwhelming the page with numerous tiles

Tile under 'Settings' page

TAKING INITIATIVE

Despite completing the initial task, I felt strongly that the pages needed improvement

To validate my self-assessment of the pages, I did a quick user interview with my mentor and 2 intern buddies (1 design, 1 SWE), who were also pretty confused.

My redesign suggestion was rejected due to priority alignment BUT I was persistent

The PM agree with my concerns but because the goal at the time was merchant acquisition, my redesign suggestion was turned down.

However, I felt really strongly about this and asked if I could do some research on my own time ontop of my current projects. From there, I took initiative to practice writing PM specs and tackling the designs.

RESEARCH

Cross-team collaboration

After several Slack calls and questions with the Payment Team, I had a solid understanding on Wish‚Äôs payout cycle. I drafted a diagram to capture a transaction‚Äôs journey from the initial purchase to ending up in the merchants bank account.

Diagram of payout cycle & transaction flow

RESEARCH

Questionaire

My next steps were to draft a rough research plan and questions to ask merchants. Afterwards, the PM helped connect me with the Business Development Team to work together and gather research. We recieved 15 responses - 8 from internal merchants (ie. Wish employees who sell on Wish) and 7 from external merchants.

Key takeaways:

  • Lack of onboarding/notifications to introduce the pages results in 3/7 of external merchants not knowing the pages existed
  • Separating pages under ‚ÄòAccount‚Äô dropdown caused merchants to think the pages are unrelated to one another
  • Current features are basic and covers only the bare minimum use cases, yet still difficult to navigate and understand


Main painpoints:
  • Merchants felt anxious and impatient when they don‚Äôt know how long until the payout is processed or paid.
  • Merchants felt angry and worried when payouts are labeled ‚Äòpaid‚Äô on MD but not recieved in their bank account (due to Wish not communicating payout must be processed by merchant‚Äôs payment provider)
  • Main pain point is the waiting period between payout being ‚Äòpaid‚Äô to when merchants actually recieve the payout in their bank account.

Narrowing down the scope and defining goals

Based on the feedback from Wish merchants and the internal team, I narrowed down 4 design goals that will help guide my decisions when designing a more intuitive and practical payment experience.

  1. Clearly show the relationship between pages
  2. Reduce the learning curve*
  3. Ease merchant anxiety when waiting for payout
  4. Consideration for future growth to make the dashboard more powerful

* One group of target users for M+ are merchants with brick-and-mortar shops transitioning to e-commerce (typically have little little to no e-commerce knowledge)

Goal 1: Show relationship between the pages

Consistent UI for cognitive recognition

By nesting the pages under the same tab in the navigation menu already intuitvely displayed the pages fall under the umbrella of ‚ÄòPayments‚Äô. I also designed the pages using similiar UI components and layout to take cognitive connection one step further.

Placement of pages on MD vs. M+

Goal 2: Reduce the learning curve

Simplify the empty states and contextually introduce information

Graphs and data is pretty daunting, especially for a new user. To prevent new users from being overwhelmed, I decided to simplify the empty states by removing elements that are irrelevent until a new user makes a transaction (ie. Y-axis, data enteries in table, etc.).

Copy text for empty states informs the user when they‚Äôll begin seeing data populate the pages or prompt them to complete to-do action items.
